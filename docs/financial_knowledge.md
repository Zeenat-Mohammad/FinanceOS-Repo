# Finlo Financial Knowledge Base

> Educational guidance only. This material is not individualized investment, tax, legal, insurance, or credit advice. Rates, laws, products, and risks vary by country and over time.

## Budgeting

A budget is a plan for directing income toward needs, wants, saving, investing, and debt repayment. A useful budget is realistic, reviewed regularly, and reconciled against actual ledger transactions.

### 50/30/20 Rule

The 50/30/20 guideline suggests up to 50% for needs, 30% for wants, and at least 20% for saving, investing, or debt reduction. Use it as a starting benchmark, not a rigid rule.

### Zero-Based Budget

Zero-based budgeting assigns every unit of expected income a job so income minus planned allocations equals zero. Savings, investments, and debt payoff count as jobs.

### Envelope Method

Envelope budgeting gives each category a fixed allowance. Digital envelopes work like physical cash envelopes: once the category is empty, pause spending or intentionally reallocate.

### Needs

Needs are essential costs such as housing, food, utilities, insurance, transport, and minimum debt payments.

### Wants

Wants are discretionary costs such as dining, entertainment, upgrades, subscriptions, and nonessential shopping.

### Budget Variance

Budget variance is the difference between planned and actual spending. Positive variance means under budget; negative variance means over budget.

### Budget Rollover

Budget rollover carries unused money into a future period. It is useful for irregular categories such as gifts, repairs, and annual renewals.

### Category Budget

A category budget is a planned spending limit for one group of transactions, such as Food, Housing, or Transportation.

### Fixed Expense

A fixed expense is predictable in timing and amount, such as rent, insurance, or a subscription.

### Variable Expense

A variable expense changes month to month, such as groceries, fuel, utilities, or shopping.

### Discretionary Spending

Discretionary spending is optional spending that can be reduced quickly when cash flow tightens.

### Budget Review

A budget review compares actual ledger transactions against the plan and adjusts next month’s allocations.

## Savings

Savings is money intentionally kept for safety, upcoming purchases, goals, or opportunities. It should be tracked separately from everyday spending capacity.

### Emergency Fund

An emergency fund is liquid money reserved for unexpected essential costs or income loss. A common target is three to six months of essential expenses.

### Starter Emergency Fund

A starter emergency fund is a small first buffer, often one pay cycle or one month of essentials, before building the full reserve.

### Sinking Fund

A sinking fund saves gradually for a known future expense such as repairs, insurance, gifts, travel, or school fees.

### Savings Rate

Savings rate measures how much income is saved or invested. Formula: savings divided by income.

### Pay Yourself First

Pay yourself first means saving automatically before discretionary spending begins.

### Goal Contribution

A goal contribution is money moved toward a specific target such as a down payment, vacation, or emergency fund.

### Cash Reserve

Cash reserve is liquid cash available for short-term needs, separate from investments and long-term goals.

### Opportunity Fund

An opportunity fund holds cash for planned chances such as investing during market declines, education, or relocation.

### No-Spend Day

A no-spend day is a day with no expense transactions. It can help build awareness and momentum.

### Savings Challenge

A savings challenge is a structured habit plan that sets small targets over days or weeks.

## Debt

Debt is money owed. Evaluate each balance by principal, APR, minimum payment, remaining term, fees, collateral, and consequences of missed payments.

### Debt Snowball

Debt snowball pays minimums on all debts and directs extra money to the smallest balance first. It creates quick wins but may cost more interest.

### Debt Avalanche

Debt avalanche pays minimums on all debts and directs extra money to the highest APR first. It usually minimizes total interest.

### Debt Consolidation

Debt consolidation replaces multiple debts with one facility. Compare total cost, fees, term length, rate type, and collateral risk before choosing it.

### Minimum Payment

Minimum payment is the required payment to keep a debt current. Paying only minimums can greatly increase payoff time and interest.

### Principal

Principal is the amount borrowed or still owed before interest and fees.

### Interest

Interest is the cost of borrowing or the return for lending capital. It depends on principal, rate, time, and compounding.

### APR

APR means annual percentage rate. It expresses annualized borrowing cost and may include certain fees.

### APY

APY means annual percentage yield. It reflects annual return after compounding.

### Simple Interest

Simple interest is calculated only on principal. Formula: principal × annual rate × years.

### Compound Interest

Compound interest earns or charges interest on prior interest. Small rate differences can become large over long periods.

### Amortization

Amortization is a payment schedule that splits each payment between interest and principal until the balance reaches zero.

### Credit Utilization

Credit utilization is credit-card balance divided by available limit. Lower utilization generally supports better credit scores.

### DTI

Debt-to-income ratio compares debt payments to income. Formula: monthly debt payments divided by monthly gross income.

### Refinance

Refinancing replaces an existing loan with a new one. Compare APR, fees, term, payment, and total repayment.

### Mortgage

A mortgage is a property-secured loan. True housing cost includes taxes, insurance, maintenance, utilities, and association fees.

### Loan Term

Loan term is the repayment duration. Longer terms usually reduce monthly payment but increase total interest.

### Secured Debt

Secured debt is backed by collateral, such as a home or vehicle.

### Unsecured Debt

Unsecured debt is not backed by specific collateral, such as many credit cards and personal loans.

## Net Worth

Net worth is assets minus liabilities. It is a snapshot of financial position, not monthly cash flow.

### Assets

Assets are things owned with economic value: cash, property, investments, vehicles, gold, and receivables.

### Liabilities

Liabilities are obligations owed: credit cards, loans, mortgages, taxes due, and unpaid bills.

### Liquid Asset

A liquid asset can be converted to cash quickly with little loss, such as bank deposits or money market funds.

### Illiquid Asset

An illiquid asset may take time or cost to sell, such as real estate, private business interests, or collectibles.

### Asset Allocation

Asset allocation divides investments among asset classes such as stocks, bonds, cash, gold, and real estate.

### Rebalancing

Rebalancing returns a portfolio to target allocation after market movements.

## Cash Flow

Cash flow measures money entering and leaving during a period. Formula: inflows minus outflows.

### Positive Cash Flow

Positive cash flow means income exceeded expenses, creating capacity for saving, investing, or extra debt payoff.

### Negative Cash Flow

Negative cash flow means expenses exceeded income, requiring reserves, borrowing, or spending reduction.

### Burn Rate

Burn rate is average monthly spending or cash use. It helps estimate runway.

### Runway

Runway is how long cash reserves can cover spending. Formula: liquid cash divided by monthly burn rate.

### Recurring Expense

A recurring expense repeats on a schedule, such as rent, insurance, subscriptions, EMIs, or phone bills.

### One-Time Expense

A one-time expense is non-recurring, such as a repair, appliance, fee, or trip.

### Transfer

A transfer moves money between owned accounts. It usually should not count as income or expense.

### Refund

A refund reverses or offsets prior spending. It may be tracked separately to avoid distorting income.

## Forecasting

Forecasting projects future income, expenses, savings, debt, investments, and cash flow from current assumptions.

### Scenario

A scenario is a what-if version of the future, such as higher rent, new income, extra debt payments, or investment contributions.

### Forecast Horizon

Forecast horizon is the number of months or years projected.

### Sensitivity

Sensitivity shows how much a result changes when an assumption changes.

### Inflation

Inflation is a general rise in prices that reduces purchasing power over time.

### CPI

CPI is a consumer price index that estimates price changes for a basket of goods and services.

### Real Return

Real return is investment return after inflation. Formula: nominal return minus inflation approximation.

### Nominal Return

Nominal return is return before adjusting for inflation.

### Monte Carlo

Monte Carlo simulation runs many possible outcomes using randomized assumptions. It estimates ranges, not guarantees.

## Investment Basics

Investing accepts uncertainty in pursuit of future growth or income. Match investments to goals, horizon, risk capacity, liquidity, costs, and taxes.

### Stock

A stock represents ownership in a company. Returns may come from price appreciation and dividends.

### ETF

An ETF is an exchange-traded fund that holds a basket of assets and trades on an exchange.

### Mutual Fund

A mutual fund pools investor money into a managed portfolio and is typically priced once per trading day.

### Index Fund

An index fund tracks a market index with rules-based holdings.

### SIP

SIP means systematic investment plan. It invests a fixed amount regularly, commonly used for mutual funds.

### REIT

A REIT is a real estate investment trust that owns or finances income-producing real estate.

### Bond

A bond is a debt security. Risks include interest-rate, inflation, credit, reinvestment, and liquidity risk.

### Gold

Gold may diversify portfolios but does not produce operating cash flow and can be volatile.

### Crypto

Crypto assets can be extremely volatile and carry custody, protocol, regulatory, and fraud risks.

### Diversification

Diversification spreads exposure across assets so one failure does not dominate the portfolio.

### Risk Tolerance

Risk tolerance is emotional comfort with volatility and possible loss.

### Risk Capacity

Risk capacity is the financial ability to take risk based on horizon, income stability, reserves, and obligations.

### Expense Ratio

Expense ratio is the annual fund fee as a percentage of assets.

### Dividend

A dividend is a company or fund distribution to investors.

### Capital Gain

A capital gain occurs when an asset sells for more than its cost basis.

### Cost Basis

Cost basis is the purchase cost adjusted for fees, splits, reinvestments, and tax rules.

### Volatility

Volatility measures price fluctuation. High volatility means values may move sharply.

### Liquidity

Liquidity is how easily an asset can be converted to cash without large price impact.

## Taxes

Tax planning means understanding taxable income, deductions, credits, withholding, filing dates, account treatment, and records. Tax law is jurisdiction-specific.

### Taxable Income

Taxable income is income after allowed exclusions, deductions, and adjustments.

### Deduction

A deduction reduces taxable income.

### Credit

A credit reduces tax owed and may be more valuable than a deduction of the same amount.

### Withholding

Withholding is tax taken from income before payment.

### Capital Gains Tax

Capital gains tax may apply when investments or assets are sold at a profit.

### Tax Loss Harvesting

Tax loss harvesting sells investments at a loss to offset taxable gains where allowed.

## Retirement

Retirement planning estimates future spending, benefits, healthcare, taxes, longevity, inflation, returns, and withdrawal risk.

### Retirement Corpus

Retirement corpus is the pool of assets intended to fund retirement spending.

### Withdrawal Rate

Withdrawal rate is annual retirement spending divided by retirement assets.

### Sequence Risk

Sequence risk is the danger that poor early retirement returns permanently damage portfolio sustainability.

### Pension

A pension is an employer or government retirement income benefit.

### Annuity

An annuity is a contract that can provide future income, often with fees and restrictions.

## Insurance

Insurance transfers defined financial risks in exchange for premiums. Choose coverage by potential loss severity and dependency.

### Premium

Premium is the amount paid for insurance coverage.

### Deductible

Deductible is the amount paid before insurance benefits apply.

### Coverage Limit

Coverage limit is the maximum the insurer may pay for a covered loss.

### Term Life Insurance

Term life insurance provides life cover for a fixed period.

### Disability Insurance

Disability insurance protects income if illness or injury prevents work.

## Credit Score

A credit score estimates credit risk using payment history, utilization, credit age, credit mix, and new inquiries.

### Payment History

Payment history tracks whether obligations were paid on time.

### Hard Inquiry

A hard inquiry is a lender credit check that may temporarily affect score.

### Credit Mix

Credit mix refers to different credit types, such as cards, installment loans, and mortgages.

## Financial Ratios

Ratios turn financial data into signals that are easier to compare over time.

### Debt-to-Income Ratio

DTI equals monthly debt payments divided by monthly income.

### Savings Rate Ratio

Savings rate equals monthly savings and investments divided by monthly income.

### Liquidity Ratio

Liquidity ratio compares liquid assets to monthly expenses.

### Emergency Ratio

Emergency ratio equals emergency savings divided by essential monthly expenses.

### Retirement Ratio

Retirement ratio compares retirement assets to target retirement corpus.

### Housing Ratio

Housing ratio compares housing costs to income.

### Investment Rate

Investment rate equals monthly investment contributions divided by income.
